Self-Introduction Email Etiquette Professional Vocabulary Formal Writing

🧑‍💼 Self-Introduction as a Freelancer Class 2

Your self-introduction is often the first impression a client has of you. A strong freelance introduction should be concise, professional, and memorable. It needs to communicate who you are, what you do, and why the client should choose you over other candidates. The key is to focus on the value you provide rather than just listing your skills.

  • Essential elements: name, profession, specialization area, years of experience, and unique value proposition
  • Opening formula: Start with “Hi, I’m [name], a [profession] specializing in [area]”
  • Value statement: Briefly explain what problems you solve for your clients
  • Social proof: Mention notable clients, projects, or results when appropriate
💬 Key Phrases
“I specialize in...” — to state your area of focus
“My expertise includes...” — to list your top skills
“I’ve been working in... for X years.” — to show experience
“I help clients achieve...” — to frame your value

✉ Email Etiquette for Freelancers Class 3

Email is the primary communication channel for most freelance relationships. Mastering email etiquette means understanding not just what to say, but how to say it. Every email you send reflects your professionalism. From the subject line to the sign-off, each component matters in building trust with clients and ensuring clear communication.

  • Subject lines: Be specific and descriptive — use formats like “Follow-up: Project X — Design Review”
  • Greeting formulas: “Dear [Name],” for formal; “Hi [Name],” for semi-formal contexts
  • Response time: Aim to reply within 24 hours; if you need more time, acknowledge receipt
  • CC/BCC rules: Only CC people who need to be informed; use BCC for group emails to protect privacy
  • Tone: Keep it professional but warm — avoid slang, excessive exclamation marks, or overly casual language
💬 Key Phrases
“I am writing to inquire about...” — formal opening for asking questions
“Please find attached...” — when including files
“Looking forward to hearing from you.” — professional closing

📧 Professional Email Structure Classes 4-5

A well-structured email follows a clear 6-part format that makes your message easy to read and act upon. This structure applies to all types of professional emails, whether you’re sending a project update, requesting information, or following up on a meeting. Understanding this format will help you write faster and more effectively.

  • Subject: Clear, specific, and includes a keyword (e.g., “Update,” “Request,” “Follow-up”)
  • Greeting: Match the formality level to your relationship with the recipient
  • Opening: State why you are writing in the first 1-2 sentences
  • Body: Provide the details, organized in short paragraphs with one idea per paragraph
  • Closing: Include a call to action — what do you need from the reader?
  • Signature: Professional sign-off with your full name and contact details

📚 Key Vocabulary for Module 1 Classes 1-5

These vocabulary words are essential for any freelance professional communicating in English. They appear frequently in emails, proposals, and client conversations. Understanding and using these terms correctly will significantly improve your professional communication.

  • Inquiry — a request for information about services or products
  • Proposal — a formal plan or offer submitted to a client
  • Deadline — the date by which work must be completed
  • Deliverables — the work products you send to a client
  • Availability — times when you are free to work or meet
  • Feedback — comments or suggestions about your work
  • Follow-up — an action to continue or check progress on something
  • Quotation — an estimate of costs for a project
  • Specification — a detailed description of requirements
  • Milestone — a significant point or stage in a project
  • Revision — a modified version of work based on feedback
  • Scope — the range or extent of work covered by a project
  • Rate — the price you charge per hour or per project

This module focuses on spoken communication with clients and everyday English. You will master phone and video call etiquette, learn how to handle freelance interviews with confidence, and build your daily routine vocabulary. Grammar focus includes the critical distinction between Present Simple and Present Continuous tenses.

Phone Calls Video Meetings Interview Skills Daily Vocabulary

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Confidently open, navigate, and close professional phone and video calls
  • Answer common freelance interview questions using the STAR method
  • Discuss your daily work routine using appropriate vocabulary and phrasing
  • Distinguish between Present Simple (habits) and Present Continuous (current actions) accurately

📞 Phone and Video Calls Class 6

Professional phone and video calls are a critical skill for freelancers who work remotely. Whether it’s an initial client consultation, a project kickoff meeting, or a quick check-in, knowing how to handle calls professionally sets you apart. This section covers every stage of a call from preparation to follow-up.

  • Opening a call: Greet the person, confirm their identity, and state your purpose clearly
  • Taking notes: Always have a notebook ready to record key points, decisions, and action items
  • Asking for clarification: Use “Could you elaborate on that?” or “Can you clarify what you mean?”
  • Summarizing: Before ending, repeat key decisions and next steps to confirm understanding
  • Closing: Thank the person, confirm next steps, and say a professional goodbye
💬 Key Phrases
“Am I speaking with [name]?” — confirming identity
“The reason I’m calling is...” — stating your purpose
“Could you elaborate on that?” — asking for more detail
“To summarize, we agreed that...” — confirming decisions
“Sorry I’m late” — polite apology when joining late

🎤 Interview Skills for Freelancers Class 7

Freelance interviews differ from traditional job interviews. Clients want to understand not just your qualifications, but how you work, communicate, and handle projects independently. Being prepared with strong answers to common questions will boost your confidence and help you win more projects.

  • STAR method: Structure answers using Situation, Task, Action, Result for behavioral questions
  • Discussing rates: Be prepared to justify your pricing with experience and value delivered
  • Portfolio presentation: Have 3-5 relevant work samples ready to discuss in detail
  • Handling rejection: Always respond professionally and ask for feedback to improve
💬 Common Interview Questions
“Tell me about yourself.” — Keep it to 60-90 seconds, focus on professional background
“What’s your rate?” — State your rate confidently and explain the value behind it
“How do you handle deadlines?” — Share your time management approach with an example
“What are your strengths?” — Pick 2-3 strengths relevant to the project

📅 Daily Routine Vocabulary Class 8

Being able to describe your daily work routine in English is essential for client communication. Clients may ask about your availability, your typical work schedule, or how you manage your time. Using natural, professional vocabulary for daily activities helps build trust and demonstrates professionalism.

  • Morning routine: wake up, get ready, grab breakfast, check emails, start work
  • Work activities: attend meetings, review documents, submit deliverables, wrap up
  • Time management: take breaks, prioritize tasks, meet deadlines, schedule calls
  • Idioms: catch up with colleagues, wind down after work, freshen up before meetings, unplug from work

📖 Grammar Focus: Present Simple vs Present Continuous Classes 8-9

Understanding when to use Present Simple versus Present Continuous is fundamental for professional communication. Using the wrong tense can confuse clients and create misunderstandings about whether you are describing a regular habit or something happening right now.

  • Present Simple (I work, she designs) — for regular habits, routines, and facts: “I check my emails every morning.”
  • Present Continuous (I am working) — for actions happening right now: “I am working on a project at the moment.”
  • Time expressions: Present Simple pairs with “every day, usually, always”; Present Continuous pairs with “currently, at the moment, right now, this week”
  • Common mistake: Don’t say “I working now” — use “I am working now”

This module develops your leadership and meeting management abilities. You will learn to lead professional meetings effectively, write meeting summaries, give and receive constructive feedback, motivate team members, and craft compelling elevator pitches that capture attention and create opportunities.

Leading Meetings Feedback Skills Team Motivation Elevator Pitches

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Lead a freelance meeting from opening to closing with confidence and professionalism
  • Write clear meeting summaries with action items, decisions, and deadlines
  • Give constructive feedback using the sandwich method and receive feedback gracefully
  • Motivate and inspire team members using recognition, goals, and autonomy

🤝 Leading Professional Meetings Class 10

Leading a meeting effectively is a core leadership skill. As a freelancer, you may need to lead client meetings, team stand-ups, or project review sessions. A well-led meeting stays on topic, respects everyone’s time, and produces clear outcomes. This section covers the essential techniques for meeting management.

  • Setting the agenda: Prepare and share an agenda before the meeting listing topics, time allocations, and objectives
  • Time management: Keep discussions on track by gently redirecting off-topic conversations
  • Encouraging participation: Use open questions to invite input from quieter members
  • Handling dominant speakers: Politely acknowledge their point and move to the next person
  • Summarizing decisions: At the end, recap key decisions and assign action items with owners and deadlines
💬 Key Phrases
“Let’s start with...” — opening the first agenda item
“Does anyone have thoughts on...?” — inviting participation
“Let’s come back to that later.” — redirecting off-topic discussion
“To summarize the key decisions...” — wrapping up the meeting

📝 Writing Meeting Summaries and Follow-Ups Class 11

A meeting summary (also called meeting minutes) is a written record of what was discussed, decided, and assigned during a meeting. Sending a summary within 24 hours of a meeting is a best practice that ensures everyone is aligned and accountable. This is especially important for freelancers managing multiple client projects.

  • Header information: Date, time, attendees, and meeting purpose
  • Key decisions: List all decisions made with brief context
  • Action items: Each task should have an owner and a deadline
  • Next steps: Date and topic for the next meeting if applicable
  • Follow-up email: Send within 24 hours with a clear subject line referencing the meeting

💬 Giving Feedback and Resolving Conflicts Class 12

Feedback is a powerful tool for improving work quality and professional relationships. As a freelancer, you may need to give feedback to clients on project requirements, or receive feedback on your deliverables. Learning to handle both sides professionally is essential for long-term success. Conflict resolution skills help you navigate disagreements constructively.

  • The Sandwich Method: Start with positive feedback, address the issue, and end with encouragement
  • Be specific: Instead of “This needs work,” say “The introduction could be more engaging by adding a hook”
  • Focus on the work, not the person: Critique the deliverable, not the individual who created it
  • Receiving feedback: Listen fully, ask clarifying questions, and thank the person before responding
💬 Key Phrases
“I noticed that...” — starting feedback observationally
“One suggestion would be...” — offering improvement ideas
“Great job on...” — acknowledging positive work
“I appreciate your feedback.” — receiving feedback professionally

🌟 Motivating and Inspiring Teams Class 13

Even as a freelancer, you may find yourself in leadership situations — managing a small team for a large project, or collaborating with other freelancers. Understanding what motivates people and how to create an inspiring work environment will make you a more effective collaborator and leader.

  • Recognition: Acknowledge contributions publicly and celebrate achievements, no matter how small
  • Clear goals: Ensure everyone understands what success looks like and how to get there
  • Autonomy: Trust team members to make decisions within their area of responsibility
  • Growth opportunities: Offer learning opportunities and challenging assignments that develop skills
  • Lead by example: Model the behavior, work ethic, and attitude you expect from others

🚀 Crafting Elevator Pitches Class 14

An elevator pitch is a concise, persuasive summary of who you are and what you offer, designed to be delivered in 30 to 60 seconds. It is called an “elevator pitch” because it should be short enough to present during a brief elevator ride. This is an invaluable tool for networking events, conferences, and client meetings.

  • Hook: Start with a surprising statistic, question, or bold statement that grabs attention
  • Problem: Identify a specific problem your target audience faces
  • Solution: Explain how you or your services solve that problem
  • Credibility: Briefly mention your experience, results, or unique qualifications
  • Call to Action: End with a clear next step — exchange contact info, schedule a meeting, etc.
💬 Example Elevator Pitch
“Hi, I’m Alex, a freelance UX designer. I help startups create intuitive mobile apps that users love. Last year, my designs helped a client increase their app engagement by 40%. I’d love to learn more about what you’re working on — can we connect on LinkedIn?”

💡 What You Will Learn

This advanced module covers higher-level business communication skills essential for growing your freelance career. You will learn to deliver persuasive presentations, negotiate rates and salaries confidently, write professional proposals and contracts, and prepare for formal job interviews with international organizations.

Presentations Negotiation Proposals Job Interviews

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Structure and deliver a professional presentation to stakeholders with visual aids and Q&A handling
  • Negotiate project rates and salary using anchoring, BATNA, and win-win strategies
  • Write comprehensive proposals and understand essential contract terms
  • Prepare for job interviews by researching companies and practicing behavioral questions

📊 Presenting to Stakeholders Class 16

Presenting to stakeholders is one of the highest-impact communication skills you can develop. Whether you are presenting a project update, a new strategy, or research findings, your ability to communicate complex information clearly and persuasively will set you apart as a professional freelancer.

  • Structure: Introduction (hook + agenda), Body (main points with evidence), Conclusion (summary + call to action)
  • Visual aids: Keep slides simple — one idea per slide, use charts and images, limit text to key phrases
  • Body language: Maintain eye contact, use open gestures, move naturally, and avoid crossing your arms
  • Handling Q&A: Listen fully, repeat the question, answer concisely, and admit when you don’t know something
💬 Key Phrases
“Today I’d like to discuss...” — opening a presentation
“As you can see from this chart...” — referencing visual data
“To conclude...” — wrapping up your presentation
“I’d like to walk you through...” — guiding the audience through content

💰 Salary and Rate Negotiation Class 17

Negotiation is a critical skill for freelancers. Many freelancers undercharge because they feel uncomfortable discussing money. Learning professional negotiation techniques will help you earn what you deserve while maintaining positive client relationships. The key is to approach negotiations as a collaborative process, not a confrontation.

  • Anchoring: State your price first when possible — the first number mentioned tends to anchor the negotiation
  • BATNA: Know your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — your walk-away point
  • Win-win approach: Frame the discussion around mutual benefit, not competition
  • Know your market rate: Research what other freelancers in your field and region charge
  • Be confident: Present your rate as justified by your experience and the value you deliver
💬 Key Phrases
“Based on the scope, I’d suggest...” — proposing a rate
“My standard rate is...” — stating your price confidently
“I’m open to discussing...” — showing flexibility
“The market rate for this is...” — using data to support your price

🎯 Job Interview Preparation Class 18

Job interviews in English can be challenging, especially for non-native speakers. Thorough preparation is the key to confidence. This section covers how to research a company, answer common behavioral questions, discuss salary expectations, and follow up professionally after the interview.

  • Research the company: Visit their website, read recent news, and understand their mission and culture
  • Behavioral questions: Prepare STAR-method answers (Situation, Task, Action, Result) for common questions
  • Salary negotiation: Research market rates, give a range rather than a single number, and emphasize total value
  • Follow-up email: Send within 24 hours thanking the interviewer and reiterating your interest

📋 Writing Proposals and Contracts Class 19

A well-written proposal can be the difference between winning and losing a project. Similarly, a clear contract protects both you and your client from misunderstandings. This section covers the essential elements of professional proposals and contracts that every freelancer should understand.

  • Proposal structure: Executive summary, scope of work, timeline, pricing, terms and conditions
  • Executive summary: A brief overview of who you are, what you propose, and why the client should choose you
  • Scope of work: Clearly define what is included and what is not included in the project
  • Contract essentials: Payment terms (net 15/30), deliverables, revision limits, confidentiality, and cancellation policy
  • Pricing: Present options (hourly, project-based, retainer) and explain what each includes

💡 What You Will Learn

This module expands your everyday English vocabulary across four essential topics: food and drinks, future plans, money, and networking. These practical topics help you feel more confident in both social and professional settings, from business lunches to networking events and financial discussions with clients.

Food & Drinks Future Plans Money Vocabulary Networking

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Order food and drinks confidently in restaurants and business settings
  • Express future plans and intentions using “will,” “going to,” and the Future Continuous
  • Discuss money matters including invoices, rates, and payment terms professionally
  • Network effectively using small talk, introductions, and follow-up techniques

🍽 Food and Drinks Vocabulary Class 20

Food and drinks vocabulary is useful in both social and professional contexts. Business meals are common when meeting clients or attending conferences. Knowing how to order, discuss dietary preferences, and make conversation around food helps you navigate these situations with ease and confidence.

  • Restaurant ordering: “I’d like to order...,” “Could I see the menu?,” “I’ll have the...”
  • Dietary restrictions: “Do you have any vegetarian options?,” “I’m allergic to...,” “Is this gluten-free?”
  • Cooking vocabulary: boil, bake, grill, fry, chop, stir, mix, serve, garnish
  • Business meals: “Shall we order some appetizers?,” “This is my treat,” “Shall we split the bill?”

🔮 Talking About Future Plans Class 21

Being able to discuss your future plans in English is important for job interviews, client meetings, and networking. Different grammar structures express different degrees of certainty and planning. Understanding these distinctions helps you communicate your intentions accurately.

  • “Will” — for spontaneous decisions, predictions, and promises: “I will send the report by Friday.”
  • “Going to” — for planned intentions: “I am going to start a new project next month.”
  • Future Continuous — for actions in progress at a future time: “This time next year, I will be running my own studio.”
  • Future Perfect — for completed actions by a future date: “By next June, I will have completed 50 projects.”
💬 Key Phrases
“I plan to...” — expressing intentions
“I’m going to start...” — definite plans
“By next year, I will have...” — future achievements

💵 Money Vocabulary Class 27

Understanding money-related vocabulary is crucial for freelancers who discuss pricing, invoicing, and budgets with clients. From quoting rates to discussing payment terms, these terms appear in almost every professional conversation about project finances.

  • Income and expenses: income, revenue, expenses, budget, savings, profit, loss
  • Freelance finances: invoice, payment, rate, currency, tax, deposit, balance
  • Payment terms: net 15, net 30, upfront payment, installment, milestone payment
  • Budgeting: “The total comes to...,” “That’s within our budget,” “We need to cut costs”
💬 Key Phrases
“The total comes to...” — stating the final amount
“Could you send the invoice?” — requesting payment documentation
“What’s your hourly rate?” — asking about pricing

🌐 Networking and Building Relationships Session 24

Networking is one of the most valuable skills for a freelancer. Many projects come through referrals and connections made at events, conferences, or online. Effective networking is not about selling — it is about building genuine relationships that create mutual value over time.

  • Small talk topics: Ask about their work, recent projects, industry trends, or the event itself
  • Introducing yourself: Use your elevator pitch as a starting point, then ask questions
  • Exchanging contact info: “Let me give you my card,” “Let’s connect on LinkedIn”
  • Following up: Send a message within 48 hours referencing something specific from your conversation
💬 Key Phrases
“What do you do?” — asking about someone’s profession
“It was great meeting you.” — ending a conversation warmly
“Let’s connect on LinkedIn.” — exchanging contact information
“I enjoyed our conversation about...” — follow-up message opening

  • Create professional invoices and discuss payment terms confidently
  • Handle crisis situations (missed deadlines, complaints) with professional communication
  • Build a personal freelance brand including portfolio, website, and social media presence

🤝 Managing Client Expectations Class 22

One of the biggest challenges freelancers face is managing what clients expect. Clear communication from the beginning of a project prevents misunderstandings, scope creep, and damaged relationships. Setting expectations is not about being rigid — it is about creating a shared understanding that protects both you and the client.

  • Setting clear scope: Define exactly what is included in the project before starting work
  • Timeline agreements: Set realistic deadlines with buffer time for unexpected issues
  • Communication frequency: Agree on how often you will provide updates (daily, weekly, bi-weekly)
  • Handling scope creep: When clients request additional work, address it professionally and discuss impact on timeline/budget
💬 Key Phrases
“As per our agreement...” — referring to the original scope
“I’d like to clarify the scope...” — addressing unclear requirements
“This falls outside the original brief.” — managing scope creep politely

📜 Invoicing and Payment Discussions Class 23

Getting paid on time is one of the most important aspects of freelance work. Professional invoicing and clear payment discussions reduce the risk of late or missed payments. Understanding how to structure invoices, set payment terms, and follow up on overdue payments is essential for maintaining a healthy cash flow.

  • Invoice structure: Your name/business, client details, invoice number, date, description of services, amount, payment terms, and bank details
  • Payment terms: Net 15 (payment within 15 days), Net 30 (within 30 days), or upfront deposit + final payment
  • Late payment follow-up: Send a polite reminder at 7 days overdue, a firmer follow-up at 14 days, and a final notice at 30 days
  • Currency discussions: Be clear about the currency, exchange rates, and who bears any conversion costs
💬 Key Phrases
“Please find the invoice attached.” — sending an invoice
“Payment is due within 30 days.” — stating payment terms
“I wanted to follow up on invoice #...” — late payment reminder

⚠️ Crisis Communication Class 25

Every freelancer will face a crisis at some point — a missed deadline, a client complaint, a technical failure, or a miscommunication. How you handle these situations defines your professional reputation. Crisis communication is about taking responsibility, offering solutions, and maintaining trust even when things go wrong.

  • Acknowledge the problem: Don’t ignore issues or make excuses — address them immediately and honestly
  • Apologize professionally: A sincere apology shows maturity: “I apologize for the delay. I understand this impacts your timeline.”
  • Propose a solution: Always come with a plan: “Here’s how I plan to resolve this...”
  • Prevent future issues: Explain what you will do differently going forward to prevent similar problems
💬 Key Phrases
“I apologize for the delay/issue...” — taking responsibility
“Here’s how I plan to resolve this...” — proposing a solution
“I take full responsibility.” — showing accountability

🎨 Building Your Freelance Brand Identity Class 26

In today’s competitive freelance market, your personal brand is what sets you apart from others offering similar services. A strong brand communicates your expertise, values, and unique value proposition. It helps clients understand who you are, what you do, and why they should work with you.

  • Personal branding: Define your niche, your unique selling points, and your target audience
  • Portfolio: Curate your best 8-12 projects that demonstrate the range and quality of your work
  • Professional website: A simple, clean website with your portfolio, services, about page, and contact form
  • Social media presence: Share your work, insights, and expertise on platforms like LinkedIn and Instagram
  • Testimonials: Collect and display client testimonials to build social proof and credibility
  • Unique value proposition: A clear statement of what makes you different and why clients should choose you

📈 Understanding the Business Cycle Classes 28-29

Understanding basic business and economic concepts helps freelancers make smarter decisions about pricing, client selection, and business strategy. The business cycle affects demand for freelance services, and knowing how to navigate different economic conditions will help you build a more resilient freelance career.

  • Supply and demand: When demand for your skills is high, you can charge more; when supply is high, you need to differentiate
  • Market trends: Stay informed about trends in your industry to anticipate changes in demand
  • Pricing strategy: Adjust your rates based on market conditions, your experience level, and the value you deliver
  • Scaling: Grow your business by hiring subcontractors, creating products, or expanding your service range
  • Diversification: Don’t rely on a single client or income stream — diversify your client base and services
💬 Key Terms
Recession — a period of economic decline with reduced business activity
Growth — expansion of the economy with increasing business opportunities
Demand — the need for your services in the market
Profit margin — the difference between your revenue and expenses
